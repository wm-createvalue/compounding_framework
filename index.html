Step 1 — Clarify the Decision
Most founders decide the outcome before they frame the decision. This step forces a clean separation: what are we actually deciding, separate from what we want the answer to be? A time horizon is mandatory input here — compounding over 18 months is a different architecture from compounding over 5 years.
What this step requires
  • A decision statement that a neutral third party could understand
  • Explicit time horizon — by when, over what period
  • Who has voice, who has vote, who is the final decision-maker
  • What would make this decision reversible vs. irreversible
Output
→ A clearly framed growth decision
Why this step matters
  • Most founders decide the outcome before they frame the decision
  • Explicit time horizon required — compounding over 18 months is a different architecture from 5 years
Step 2 — Map the "What If"
Explicitly naming team, cash, and focus as second-order ripple vectors forces the uncomfortable questions early — before commitment makes them expensive to ask. Most frameworks ask "what if it works?" — this one asks "what breaks, and for whom?"
What this step requires
  • Best case, worst case, and most likely case modelled explicitly
  • Hidden assumptions surfaced and named (not left implicit)
  • Second-order effects on: team capacity, cash runway, strategic focus
  • Opportunity cost — what are we not doing if we do this?
Output
→ A structured scenario map
Why this step matters
  • Second-order thinking surfaces what most leaders discover too late
  • Forces separation of assumptions from evidence before commitment
Step 3 — Apply Financial Reality
Anchoring the decision in real numbers prevents commitment to strategies that cannot survive contact with a balance sheet. Cash requirements, time-to-return, and break-even thresholds must be stated before a decision proceeds.
What this step requires
  • Cash required to reach the next value milestone
  • Time-to-return estimate with a low and high range
  • Break-even threshold — what does success minimally look like?
  • Sensitivity: what variable, if wrong, kills this decision?
Output
→ Financial demands & returns view
Why this step matters
  • Prevents committing capital to strategies built on hope, not modelling
  • The single-variable sensitivity test reveals where the decision is most fragile
Step 4 — Test for Compounding
The most distinctive step. Asking "does value accumulate or reset each cycle?" is a powerful filter most decision frameworks entirely lack. It reframes growth as architecture, not activity. The live Decision Scorer is the tool that makes this test quantifiable and reproducible.
The compounding test
  • Does this create leverage — systems, brand, capability — that multiplies?
  • Does value sustain beyond the initial investment cycle?
  • Does this open more future choices (optionality) or close them?
  • Does this make the business more resilient through downturns?
  • Does this increase intrinsic business value, not just revenue?
Output
→ Score: Go / Redesign / No
Why this step matters
  • "Redesign" is a real output — most frameworks force a binary that discards good ideas prematurely
  • The scorer turns this into a quantified, reproducible verdict
Step 5 — Commit with Eyes Open
Commitment without a closing ritual collapses into gut feel. This step provides four specific conditions that must be satisfied before any commitment is made: reversibility, explicit conditions, pre-mortem, and review trigger.
The closing ritual
  • Reversibility rating: one-way door vs. two-way door
  • Explicit commitment: what are we actually agreeing to?
  • Pre-mortem: if this fails in 12 months, what was the most likely cause?
  • Review trigger: what event prompts a reassessment?
Output
→ Aligned decision + next steps
Why this step matters
  • Distinguishes one-way doors from two-way doors, calibrating commitment depth appropriately
  • Team leaves with a shared, explicit understanding of what was agreed and why

Durability tests whether the value survives. If benefits disappear the moment investment stops, the decision resets — not compounds.

  1. Will the benefits of this decision still be felt in three years without ongoing, heavy reinvestment?
  2. Does this create switching costs, habits, or network effects that make the value sticky over time?
  3. Is the core value of this decision protected from easy competitor replication within 18 months?
  4. Would a significant market downturn reduce the value of this decision, or leave it largely intact?
  5. Does this decision strengthen a relationship, reputation, or capability that compounds with time rather than depreciating?
Optionality 15%
5/10
▾

Optionality measures whether this decision opens or closes future choices. A high optionality decision keeps you in the game for moves you haven't thought of yet.

  1. Does committing to this decision preserve our ability to pursue at least two other significant opportunities in the next 12 months?
  2. If this doesn't work as planned, does it leave us with assets, relationships, or learnings that remain useful?
  3. Does this decision give us access to a new market, channel, or customer segment we currently can't reach?
  4. Is this decision reversible within six months if circumstances change — without unacceptable cost?
  5. Will executing this decision give us better information to make the next decision more confidently?

Resilience tests whether this decision strengthens the business's ability to absorb shocks — or increases its fragility under pressure.

  1. If revenue dropped by 30% for six months, would this decision still look like the right one to have made?
  2. Does this reduce our dependency on a single client, supplier, channel, or technology?
  3. Would this decision make our team stronger, more capable, or more aligned — not just busier?
  4. Does this give us greater pricing power, margin protection, or cost efficiency over time?
  5. If a key competitor copied this decision within 12 months, would we still have a meaningful advantage?

Methodology Foundations

Built on validated thinking.
Sharpened for founders.

The Compounding Decision Framework draws on decades of validated research in decision science, strategic theory, and behavioural economics — synthesised into a single, actionable process.

⟁
The Core Insight
Most business decisions are evaluated on short-term return. This framework introduces a different primary filter: does this decision compound? Does it build leverage, durability, and strategic optionality over time — or does it produce activity that resets at the end of each cycle? That single question changes what gets built.
What makes this framework distinctive
✓
The compounding lens as a primary filter. Most decision frameworks optimise for return. This one optimises for compounding architecture — asking whether value accumulates or resets, and treating that distinction as the most important question before committing capital or focus.
✓
Second-order effects are explicit, not assumed. Team capacity, cash runway, and strategic focus are named as required inputs — not afterthoughts. The framework surfaces what most leaders discover too late.
✓
Three outputs, not two. Go / Redesign / No. "Redesign" is the most valuable output — it tells you the idea has merit but the structure does not. Most frameworks force a binary that discards good ideas prematurely.
✓
Financial reality is a mandatory gate, not a footnote. Cash requirements, time-to-return, and break-even thresholds must be stated before a decision proceeds — preventing commitment to strategies that cannot survive contact with a balance sheet.
Validated intellectual foundations
①
Compounding & mental models — Charlie Munger. Munger's latticework of mental models and his emphasis on compounding as the central force in building durable value is foundational to Step 4's compounding test and the five-lens scoring architecture.
②
Second-order thinking — Howard Marks. Marks' framework for thinking beyond first-order consequences — "and then what?" — directly informs Step 2's scenario mapping, particularly the requirement to surface ripple effects on team, cash, and focus.
③
Optionality & asymmetric risk — Nassim Taleb. Taleb's concept of seeking decisions with convex payoffs — limited downside, large upside — underpins the Optionality dimension of the scorer and the reversibility rating in Step 5.
④
Pre-mortem & decision hygiene — Daniel Kahneman. Kahneman's work on cognitive bias and prospective hindsight (the pre-mortem technique) shapes Step 5's closing ritual — specifically the requirement to name the most likely failure cause before committing.
Additional validated frameworks this methodology builds on
⑤
Jobs-to-be-Done — Clayton Christensen. The JTBD lens informs Step 1's decision clarification process: what is the real job this decision is hired to do? Separating the surface request from the underlying strategic need.
⑥
Durable competitive advantage — Michael Porter. Porter's work on sustainable competitive positioning grounds the Durability and Value dimensions — asking whether a decision builds structural advantage or merely temporary differentiation.
⑦
Reversible vs. irreversible decisions — Jeff Bezos. The one-way/two-way door distinction from Amazon's decision-making doctrine is the direct source of Step 5's reversibility rating — calibrating commitment depth to the actual stakes involved.
⑧
Sensitivity analysis — financial modelling practice. The requirement in Step 3 to identify the single variable whose failure kills the decision draws on standard sensitivity analysis methodology used in venture capital and corporate finance.
